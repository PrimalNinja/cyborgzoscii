Morning in Morwell starts quietly. The gum trees along the creek keep the same patient watch they’ve held for decades, leaves whispering when the breeze gets brave enough to move through them. Magpies test their notes against the sky, staking melodic claims before the day warms up. Out past the main road, fog sits low in the paddocks like spilled milk, softening fence lines and making the world feel smaller, more private. You can smell eucalyptus and damp earth, that mix that tells you it rained overnight even if you slept through it. The bakery on Commercial Road has been awake since 4am. Dough has been kneaded, proved, and turned into loaves with crusts that crackle when you squeeze them. Coffee hisses, locals trade nods, and the front window fogs from the inside out. School kids cut through the park, backpacks bouncing, negotiating trades of fruit boxes and freedom. At the station, the Traralgon train idles for thirty seconds like it always does, doors sighing open then shut, taking a handful of commuters toward the city and leaving the platform empty again. Latrobe Valley’s power stations hum in the distance, a low industrial bass note under the birdsong. They’re part of the landscape here, as much as the hills or the old mine benches now greening over. By midmorning the fog lifts and the sky goes broad and blue, the kind of blue that makes distances honest. You see the ridge lines, the far farms, the places you’ll get to later. The library opens and the same three regulars take their corners. The op shop sorts yesterday’s donations. Someone is always mowing somewhere. Wind picks up after lunch, shaking the last drops off the trees and sending bin lids clapping. Storm clouds might build over the Baw Baws, or they might not. Weather here keeps its own counsel. Late afternoon turns everything gold. The creek goes mirror-bright, dogs chase balls with more optimism than aim, and the bakery sells the last of the day-old rolls for half price. Dusk doesn’t rush. It settles, room by room, street by street, until the magpies try one last chorus and the first stars pin themselves up. Tomorrow, it all starts again.
